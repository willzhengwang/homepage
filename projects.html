Introduction

Ground-Based Synthetic Aperture Radar (GBSAR) is a remote sensing technology that is able to monitor natural and artificial surfaces with areal extents of up to a few square kilometres and offers the measurement of surface displacements in the radar Line Of Sight (LOS) with sub-mm precision (Caduff et al, 2015). GBSAR also has inherent advantages in terms of portability, view-angle to steep slopes, and flexibility for data collection over spaceborne SAR. Consecutive GBSAR acquisitions with a high temporal resolution (several seconds) enable time series analysis of fast-changing scenarios and monitoring of hazardous areas in real time that can act as the basis for early warning systems. The aforementioned characteristics of GBSAR make it unique and complementary to other deformation monitoring techniques (e.g. levelling, global navigation satellite systems, photogrammetric computer vision and terrestrial laser scanning), thereby offering significant potential in the prediction and mitigation of hazards.
